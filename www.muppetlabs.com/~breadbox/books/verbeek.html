Gustave Verbeek, Little Lady Lovekins, and Old Man Muffaroo

Gustave Verbeek [born Gustave Verbeck] (1867-1937), drew a Sunday comic strip named "The Upside-Downs of Little Lady Lovekins and Old Man Muffaroo" for The New York Herald. All in all 65 strips were created, running from October 4, 1903 to January 15, 1905. This unusual strip was a half-page in size, giving him six panels, but Verbeek created twelve panels of story, by the remarkable expedient of requiring the reader to turn the page upside down halfway through. The six panels, turned upside-down, would then illustrate the second half of the narrative.

List of Collections

Note: This web page was originally compiled back when no complete collection existed, and in fact many episodes were simply unavailable, except in newspaper archives. As of 2009, this is no longer the case, as Sunday Press Books provides a beautiful and fully comprehensive anthology (see below). I have left this page up simply for the sake of posterity.

The following list is all the published collections of Verbeek's unusual comic strip, to the best of my knowledge. I welcome corrections and additions to the information on this page. Items marked with a <*> are ones that I have in my personal collection. Information about the books that I do not have in my personal collection typically comes from obscure sources, and may not be accurate. Please contact me if you can verify or correct any information about these books in particular.

  • The Incredible Upside-Downs of Little Lady Lovekins and Old Man Muffaroo. Published by G.W. Dillingham Company (New York) in 1905. Library of Congress catalog number PN6161 .V4 fol. The book is 11½" tall by 17¼" wide. It is a paperback, with a tape-and-staple binding. The cover is of the same (heavy) paper as the pages of the book. This is the original collection. It includes 24 strips, which are reproduced in full size and in color. No ancillary text is included, not even a copyright page (brief copyright information is printed on the front cover). The strips' original banners have been replaced with new banners that just show the title of the episode (omitting the name of the strip itself). In one or more cases the title is changed slightly from its original publication. A few of the strips are missing copyright notices. <*>
    Note: Most sources state that there are 25 strips in this book. My copy only contains 24 strips, however. It is possible that my copy is missing a strip, as it isn't in the best of conditions, but for the time I am assuming that the popular report of 25 is simply an error. If anyone has access to an intact copy of this book, I would appreciate any information.
  • The Incredible Upside-Downs of Gustave Verbeek. Published by The Rajah Press (Box 23; Summit, NJ) in 1963. Library of Congress catalog number NC1429.V52 A47. The book is 9¼" tall by 12½" wide. The cover is brown cardstock, with a stapled binding, and shows two copies of the roc image (from "A Fish Story"), one in each orientation. The book's introduction is written by George M. Naimark. The book contains 20 strips, one per page arranged in two rows of three panels each. The strips were reproduced from library microfilms of the original newspapers. They are black-and-white, and do not appear to have been altered (other than having been reduced). <*>
    • Same, second printing. Some or all of the copies in this run came with two pages, cut to the same size as the book, laid into the front. These pages listed excerpts from several reviews of the book. <*>
  • "The Upside-Down World of Gustave Verbeek". An article in Linus, issue no. 0, May 1970, published by Milano Libri Edizioni s.r.l. (1 Via Spiga; Milan 20121; Italy). The magazine was an English companion to an Italian magazine of the same name. The magazine is 10¾" tall and 8¼" wide. The article is a 16-page supplement, appearing between pages 42 and 43, and is printed on cheaper paper than the rest of the magazine. The text of the article is written by Frank Dickens, editor of the magazine. The article contains 15 strips, each a full page in size (three rows of two panels, printed sideways). The strips are black-and-white, and do not appear to have been altered (other than having been reduced to fit on the page). All of the strips included appear also in the Real Free Press collection. <*>
  • The Incredible Upside-Downs (of Gustave Verbeek). Published by Real Free Press International (Oude Niewstraat 10; Amsterdam; Netherlands) in 1973. No catalog number is provided. The book is 9¼" tall by 12½" wide. It is a paperback, with a stapled binding. The front cover shows the upside-down roc image, colored by Joost Swarte. The back cover shows the complete strip "The Fairy Palace", also colored. The introductory pages are the same as that of the Rajah Press edition (modulo some rearranging). The book proper contains 26 black-and-white strips, one per page arranged in two rows of three panels each. The first 19 strips are the same as the Rajah Press edition, minus "The Fairy Palace". The title page credits George M. Naimark, Marcel Just, and R. Olaf Stoop (president of The Real Free Press Foundation) as the collectors who provided the book's contents. <*>
  • The Incredible Upside-Downs of Gustave Verbeek. Published by Nostalgia Press, Incorporated (Box 293; Franklin Square, NY 11010) in 1976. ISBN 0911204016. This book is 13½" tall by 9½" wide. It is a hardback, and the cover shows the (colored) upside-down roc image. The spine reads "Gustave Verbeek / The Incredible Upside-Downs". The book's introduction is written by David A. Kaler, and dated July 1, 1976. The book contains 26 strips, one per page. The panels have been arranged into three rows of two panels each. The strips are the same as the ones from the Real Free Press edition, without "The Fairy Palace". The strips are colored, but they are not the original coloring. The colorist is not identified. The original captions have been excised and replaced with typeset captions. The copyright notices have likewise been removed. The banners, which show the strip's full name and the title of the individual episode, are occasionally altered, presumably to fit above two panels instead of three. One episode, "The Little Lady is Naughty Again", has also had text removed from a sign that appears in one panel. <*>
  • Niet Te Geloven Ondersteboven!. Published by Erven Thomas Rap (Spuistraat 283; Amsterdam; Netherlands) in 1976. ISBN 9060051106. The book is 9¼" tall by 12½" wide. It is a paperback, with a stapled binding. The contents are essentially the same as the Real Free Press edition, except that all text has been translated to Dutch (including Naimark's introduction). The translation was done by Jan Donkers, and the new captions were lettered by Charlotte Fisher. The banners of the strips have likewise been replaced with Dutch versions. The name of the strip is rendered as "Lovekins en Muffaroo". The front cover shows a crudely colored version of the upside-down roc image. The back cover is identical to that of the Real Free Press edition (modulo the translated text). <*>
  • Dessus-Dessous. Published by Pierre Horay (22 bis Passage Dauphine Paris 6e; France) in 1978. ISBN 2705800778. This book is 12½" tall by 9½" wide. It is a hardback. The cover shows the same image as the Nostalgia Press book. The spine, oddly enough, reads "Pierre Horay / Dessus-Dessous / Gustave Verbeck". (The cover and the title page show Gustave Verbeek.) The book's introduction is written by Pierre Couperie, who also did the translation. The book contains 26 strips. They are the same strips as are in the Nostalgia Press edition, except that the captions have been translated into French. The banners likewise have been replaced by (typeset) French translations. The name of the strip is rendered as "Lady Lovekins et Pere Muffaroo". Included in the introduction are two small but unaltered reproductions of "The Fairy Palace", the second copy printed upside-down. <*>
  • Four Confusing Tales, Each Illustrated by Six Up-Turnable Pictures, from the Incredible Topsy-Turvy World of Gustave Verbeek. Published and distributed by Tobar Limited (St. Margaret, Harleston, Norfolk IP20 0PJ; UK), year unknown (mid- to late-1990s). ISBN 1903230004. This book is 5¾" tall and 4¼" wide. It is really a pamphlet instead of a book: The pages are all cardstock, with a stapled binding. The cover shows two sample images, along with their upside-down versions. The book's introduction is not credited, nor is the source of the strips. The book contains four strips, with each panel on a separate page. The strips are in color; they are taken directly from the original 1905 collection. The original captions have all been replaced with typeset captions, placed outside of the panels. The banners are not included; rather, the title of each episode appears at the top of the pages that comprise it. Arrows and directions have been added to let the reader know when to turn the booklet upside down and to start going "backwards" through the pages, and afterwards what page to turn to for the next strip. <*>
  • "Gustave Verbeek: The Forgotten Genius (Upside-Down Visions, Rightside-Up Delights)". An article in Nemo: The Classic Comics Library (ISSN 07469438), issue no. 10, December 1984, published by Fantagraphics Books, Incorporated (707 Camino Manzanas; Thousand Oaks, CA 91360). The magazine is 10¾" tall by 8¼" wide. The article is part of a series called "Fantasy in the Comics", and appears on pp. 21-38. The text of the article is written by Richard Marschall, who is presumably also the collector who supplied the strips. The article contains 17 strips. One strip ("The Boy and the Bean-Stalk") appears in the introduction and is reduced to half-page size; the others are each a full page in size (three rows of two panels, printed sideways). The strips are black-and-white and, except for reduction, appear to be completely unaltered. <*>
  • Unten ist Oben. Published by Comic Companie (Siesmayerstraße 10, 6000 Frankfurt/Main 1; Germany) in 1985. ISBN 3820101802. The book is 13½" tall by 9½" wide. It is a hardback. The cover shows the same image as the Nostalgia Press book. Angela Praesent is credited with the book's translation. There are 26 strips, the same strips as are in the Nostalgia Press edition, except that the captions have been translated into German. The name of the strip is rendered as "Unten ist Oben mit Little Lady Lovekins und Old Man Muffaroo". The banners likewise have been replaced with German versions. (Unlike the typeset banners of the French version, these banners reflect the overall variety of lettering styles used in the originals.) The introduction is a translation of Naimark's introduction for the Rajah Press book. <*>
  • 少女ラブキンズとマファルー老人の冒険 (The Incredible Upside-Downs of Gustave Verbeek). Published by TBS Publications, Tokyo in 1987. ISBN 4782510713. Edited by 坂根厳夫 (Itsuo Sakane). No further information about this book is currently available to me.
  • The Upside-Down World of Gustave Verbeek. Published by Sunday Press Books (450 Monroe Drive; Palo Alto, CA 94306) in 2009. ISBN 0976888572. This book is 10½" tall by 16" wide. It is a hardback, and the cover shows an image of Muffaroo and Lovekins. The book is edited by Peter Maresca, and includes introductory texts by Martin Gardner and Jeet Heer. Unlike all previous collections, this anthology contains the complete set of episodes, in full size and with the original coloring preserved. The book also contains episodes of Verbeek's other comic strips "The Loony Lyrics of Lulu" and "The Tiny Tads", as well as reproductions of much of Verbeek's serious art. The book is beautiful and comes highly recommended (website). <*>

The following list is of publications in which noteworthy individual strips appear, outside of what is already covered in the above list.

  • In the Shadow of No Towers, by Art Spiegelman. Published by Pantheon books in 2004. ISBN 0375423079. In the back of the book are images of several early comic strips. Plate III shows a color reproduction of "The Fairy Palace". This is the first full-sized, unaltered reproduction of one of Verbeek's strips to be published since 1905. It is also worth noting that a small Old Man Muffaroo (or is it Lady Lovekins?) can be seen on the cover. <*>
  • The Comics Before 1945, by Brian Walker. Published by Harry N. Abrams in 2004. ISBN 0810949709. On page 51 is a color reproduction of "The Thrilling Adventure of the Dragon". The reproduced image is 6½" tall by 9" wide.
  • "The Comics That Time Forgot", by Peter Maresca. An article in Comic Art (ISSN 15427447), issue no. 7, winter 2005, published by Comic Art (5715 Nottingham Ave.; St. Louis, MO 63109), pp. 46-57. The article reproduces sixteen different Sunday comics from the early 20th century. On p. 52 is a color reproduction of "The Wonderful Cure of the Waterfall". The reproduced image is 5" tall by 7" wide. <*>
  • Art Out of Time: Unknown Comics Visionaries, 1900-1969, by Dan Nadel. Published by Harry N. Abrams, Inc. in 2006. ISBN 0810958384. On pages 273-275 are color reproduction of six episodes. The reproductions are 4¾" tall by 6" wide, and are printed two to a page. <*>
